# China's key government institutions for AI and language education

**The Ministry of Education (教育部) dominates both AI and language education policy in China**, serving as the primary drafter, implementer, and enforcer of curricula, standards, and reform. For AI education, it shares the landscape with the Ministry of Science and Technology and the Cyberspace Administration of China. For language education, its embedded State Language Commission and subordinate Center for Language Education and Cooperation handle domestic standardization and international promotion respectively. Below is a detailed breakdown of each institution, organized by topic.

---

## Part 1: AI education policy institutions

### Ministry of Education — the central driver of AI curricula

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **English name** | Ministry of Education of the People's Republic of China |
| **Chinese name** | 中华人民共和国教育部 |
| **Website** | https://www.moe.gov.cn/ (Chinese) · http://en.moe.gov.cn/ (English) |
| **Current Minister** | Huai Jinpeng (怀进鹏) |

The MOE is the **single most important institution for AI education in China**. It directly controls curricula at every level—primary, secondary, and higher education—and has issued every major AI education directive since 2018. Its Department of Science, Technology and Informatization (科学技术与信息化司) handles AI education specifically, while a dedicated National Expert Committee on Education Digitalization advises on strategy.

**Notable policies and initiatives:**

The MOE's **AI Innovation Action Plan for Institutions of Higher Education** (April 2018) was the foundational policy document, mandating "AI+X" interdisciplinary programs at universities, expanding AI doctoral enrollment, and setting a 2030 target for world-leading university AI research. In February 2024, it designated **184 primary and secondary schools as national AI education pilot bases**. Then in November 2024, it released landmark guidance integrating AI education into all K-12 grade levels: awareness activities for lower primary students, understanding and application for upper primary and junior high, and project-based creation for senior high school. In 2025, the MOE launched the **Smart Education of China 2.0 platform** with embedded AI functionalities across 13 disciplines, published a Guide to Using Generative AI in Primary and Secondary Schools, and co-issued with eight other departments an April 2025 guideline on accelerating education digitalization that calls for education-specific large language models. By September 2025, mandatory AI education began rolling out nationwide, with all primary and secondary schools required to offer a minimum of **8 class hours of AI per year**. The MOE's own white paper declared 2025 the "inaugural year of China's smart education initiative."

**Why the MOE matters most (80–100 word paragraph):** The Ministry of Education is China's most important institution for AI education, directly overseeing curricula, university programs, and teacher training nationwide. Since 2018, it has issued the AI Innovation Action Plan for Higher Education, mandating "AI+X" interdisciplinary programs and expanding AI doctoral enrollment. In November 2024, it released groundbreaking guidance integrating AI education into all K-12 grade levels, and designated 184 pilot schools as AI education bases. The MOE launched the Smart Education of China 2.0 platform and in 2025 released guidelines for generative AI use in schools, making it the central driver of China's AI talent pipeline.

### Ministry of Science and Technology — the strategic architect

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **English name** | Ministry of Science and Technology of the People's Republic of China |
| **Chinese name** | 中华人民共和国科学技术部 (commonly 科技部) |
| **Website** | https://www.most.gov.cn/ (Chinese) · http://en.most.gov.cn/ (English) |

MOST does not manage schools or curricula, but it **shapes the strategic and ethical framework** within which AI education operates. It led the drafting of the foundational **New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan** (新一代人工智能发展规划), issued by the State Council in July 2017 as Document No. 35. That plan mentions "talent" 34 times and explicitly calls for AI courses in primary and secondary schools, the establishment of AI first-level disciplines, and cultivation of "compound talent" mastering interdisciplinary AI knowledge.

**Notable policies and initiatives:**

MOST established the **AI Strategic Advisory Committee** and **AI Planning and Promotion Office** (2017–2019), the first institutions solely dedicated to overseeing national AI strategy. In March 2019, it created the **National New Generation AI Governance Expert Committee** (chaired by Xue Lan of Tsinghua University), which published China's first AI governance principles in June 2019—eight principles including harmony, fairness, and agile governance—and followed with **Ethical Norms for New Generation AI** in September 2021. MOST's December 2023 **Administrative Measures for Science and Technology Ethics Review** became China's first unified ethics-review regulation, requiring all universities and research institutions in AI to establish ethics review committees. MOST also controls the distribution of national research funding for foundational AI science and implements the overseas high-level talent recruitment plan targeting foreign AI researchers.

**Why MOST matters (80–100 word paragraph):** The Ministry of Science and Technology is China's strategic architect for AI development and ethics governance, with direct relevance to AI education through talent policy and research funding. MOST led the creation of the landmark 2017 New Generation AI Development Plan, which explicitly mandated AI curriculum development across all education levels and set 2030 targets for world-leading AI talent centers. It established the National New Generation AI Governance Expert Committee in 2019, publishing China's first AI ethics principles and norms. Its 2023 Ethics Review Measures require universities and research institutions to establish AI ethics committees, directly shaping how AI research and education are conducted nationwide.

### Cyberspace Administration of China — the regulatory gatekeeper

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **English name** | Cyberspace Administration of China (also: Office of the Central Cyberspace Affairs Commission) |
| **Chinese name** | 国家互联网信息办公室 (also 中央网络安全和信息化委员会办公室) |
| **Website** | https://www.cac.gov.cn/ |

The CAC is not an education body, but its regulatory authority over AI services **directly determines which AI tools can be used in Chinese classrooms**. As the lead regulator for algorithms, generative AI, and online content, the CAC sets the compliance framework that educational technology companies must navigate.

**Notable policies and initiatives:**

The CAC was the lead drafter of the **Interim Measures for the Management of Generative AI Services** (effective August 15, 2023), co-signed by six other agencies including the MOE, MOST, and NDRC. This was China's first binding regulation on generative AI, governing ChatGPT-type tools with provisions directly relevant to education: Article 2 notably exempts educational and scientific research institutions developing AI not provided to the public. The CAC also issued the **Administrative Provisions on Algorithm Recommendation Services** (March 2022), the **Administrative Provisions on Deep Synthesis** (January 2023), and **AI Content Labeling Measures** (effective September 1, 2025) mandating identification of AI-generated content—all of which affect educational materials and platforms. Its algorithm filing system has processed thousands of filings, including educational AI applications.

**Why the CAC matters (80–100 word paragraph):** The Cyberspace Administration of China is the dominant regulator of AI services, directly impacting how AI tools are used in Chinese education. As lead drafter of the 2023 Interim Measures for Generative AI Services—co-signed by the Ministry of Education and five other agencies—the CAC sets the compliance framework for AI chatbots, tutoring platforms, and content generation tools used in schools and universities. Its algorithm filing system and 2025 content labeling requirements shape the educational AI ecosystem. The regulations exempt educational institutions developing AI for internal research while mandating strict oversight of AI services provided to students and the public.

### Other relevant institutions for AI education

**National Development and Reform Commission** (国家发展和改革委员会 / https://www.ndrc.gov.cn/) coordinates the State Council's August 2025 **"AI Plus" initiative**, a decade-long roadmap that explicitly includes education transformation and targets **70% AI penetration across key sectors by 2027**. The NDRC co-issued the 2020 directive accelerating AI graduate cultivation at "Double First-Class" universities and aligns AI education targets with Five-Year Plans.

**Ministry of Industry and Information Technology** (工业和信息化部 / https://www.miit.gov.cn/) directly oversees seven major technical universities (including Harbin Institute of Technology and Beijing Institute of Technology), published the 2024 National AI Industry Standardisation Guidelines targeting **50+ AI standards by 2026**, and runs talent programs targeting high-level AI specialists.

The **State Council** (国务院 / https://www.gov.cn/) sits atop the hierarchy. Its July 2017 New Generation AI Development Plan catalyzed the entire AI education ecosystem, and its August 2025 "AI+" Opinions set the most ambitious targets yet: full intelligent economy by 2035, with education explicitly identified as a key integration sector.

## Part 2: Language education policy institutions

### Ministry of Education — the authority over all language curricula

The MOE is equally dominant in language education. It mandates **English as one of three compulsory gaokao subjects** (alongside Chinese and mathematics), worth **150 out of 750 total points**. Since 2001, English has been required from Grade 3 in primary school. The MOE sets national syllabi through its Advisory Committee of College Foreign Language Teaching, which published the authoritative **Guidelines on College English Teaching** (2020 final version), requiring non-English-major university students to complete approximately **16 credits (~280 teaching hours)** of English.

**Notable policies and initiatives:**

The **2014 Gaokao Reform** (State Council implementation guidelines) introduced the "3+1+2" model now used in most provinces, under which students may take the English test twice a year with the higher score counting. Students may also choose Japanese, Russian, German, French, or Spanish instead of English—approximately **5% now do so**. The July 2021 **"Double Reduction" policy** (双减政策) banned for-profit after-school academic tutoring for K-9 students, devastating the estimated $100-billion tutoring industry and significantly affecting English learning access. Despite recurring public proposals to downgrade English in the gaokao, **no national-level policy has reduced English's weight as of early 2026**. The reform focus has been procedural (multiple test attempts) rather than substantive reductions in importance.

**Why the MOE matters for language education (80–100 word paragraph):** The Ministry of Education is the supreme authority over language education in China, setting curricula, examination standards, and policy for both foreign language learning and Chinese language instruction at every level. It mandates English as a compulsory gaokao subject worth 150 of 750 points, requires English instruction from Grade 3, and publishes national college English teaching guidelines. The MOE also houses the State Language Commission and oversees the Center for Language Education and Cooperation. Its 2021 "Double Reduction" policy reshaped the language tutoring landscape, while the ongoing gaokao reform now allows students to sit the English exam twice per year.

### State Language Commission — guardian of Putonghua and standards

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **English name** | State Language Commission (National Language and Script Working Committee) |
| **Chinese name** | 国家语言文字工作委员会 (abbreviated 国家语委) |
| **Website** | http://www.moe.gov.cn/jyb_sy/China_Language/ (hosted within the MOE site since 2019) |

The SLC is China's official language regulator, a vice-ministerial-level bureau that merged into the MOE in 1998 under a "one institution, two names" (一个机构两块牌子) arrangement. The Minister of Education concurrently serves as SLC Director. The Commission traces its origins to the **Chinese Character Reform Committee** established in 1954, which developed the Hanyu Pinyin scheme (approved 1958) and the Simplified Character scheme (1956).

**Notable policies and initiatives:**

The SLC enforces the **2001 Law on the Standard Spoken and Written Language**, which mandates Putonghua in education, media, and government, achieving over **80% Putonghua proficiency nationwide**. It administers the **Putonghua Proficiency Test** (普通话水平测试). In 2013, it published the **Table of General Standard Chinese Characters** (通用规范汉字表) containing **8,105 characters**. Critically for English education, the SLC co-issued with the MOE **China's Standards of English Language Ability (CSE)** in April 2018—China's first national English proficiency scale with 9 levels across 3 stages and approximately 3,000 "can-do" descriptors, linked to international frameworks including CEFR, IELTS, and TOEFL iBT. A **revised CSE** was released November 26, 2024, effective March 1, 2025, adding new scales for interpreting, translation, and typical language activities.

**Why the SLC matters (80–100 word paragraph):** The State Language Commission is China's official language regulator, responsible for standardizing Putonghua and Chinese characters nationwide under the 2001 Language Law. A vice-ministerial bureau merged into the MOE since 1998, the SLC developed foundational systems including Hanyu Pinyin and simplified characters. It co-issued China's Standards of English Language Ability in 2018—the country's first unified nine-level English proficiency framework aligned with CEFR, IELTS, and TOEFL—and released a revised edition effective March 2025. The SLC administers the Putonghua Proficiency Test and has achieved over 80% national Putonghua proficiency, making it essential to both Chinese and English language standardization.

### Center for Language Education and Cooperation — the international arm

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **English name** | Center for Language Education and Cooperation (CLEC) |
| **Chinese name** | 中外语言交流合作中心 |
| **Website** | https://www.chinese.cn/ |
| **Predecessor** | Hanban / Office of Chinese Language Council International (国家汉语国际推广领导小组办公室), established 1987 |

CLEC is the **direct successor to Hanban**, renamed in July 2020 as part of a major reorganization that separated government operations from the Confucius Institute brand. It is a non-profit professional educational institution directly under the MOE, tasked with providing Chinese language and cultural teaching resources worldwide. CLEC manages international teacher dispatch, teaching materials, the **"Chinese Bridge" (汉语桥)** proficiency competitions, and coordinates with the HSK testing system (now administered by CTI Co., Ltd.) under the upgraded "Three Stages, Nine Levels" framework.

The Confucius Institute brand and operations were transferred to the **Chinese International Education Foundation** (中国国际中文教育基金会 / CIEF), a nominally independent non-governmental organization established July 5, 2020, registered with the Civil Affairs Ministry and accessible at https://www.cief.org.cn/. CIEF manages approximately **496 Confucius Institutes and 757 Confucius Classrooms** across over 160 countries, though many US-based institutes have closed amid political controversy.

**Why CLEC matters (80–100 word paragraph):** The Center for Language Education and Cooperation is the MOE's primary vehicle for promoting Chinese language education internationally. Renamed from Hanban in July 2020 to separate government functions from the Confucius Institute brand, CLEC dispatches Chinese language teachers worldwide, provides teaching materials, hosts the prestigious "Chinese Bridge" competitions for non-native speakers, and coordinates Chinese proficiency testing. It works alongside the Chinese International Education Foundation, which now manages roughly 496 Confucius Institutes in over 160 countries. CLEC's reorganization reflected China's effort to professionalize international Chinese education while distancing it from the political controversies surrounding Confucius Institutes in Western nations.

### National Education Examinations Authority — the testing infrastructure

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **English name** | National Education Examinations Authority (NEEA) |
| **Chinese name** | 教育部教育考试院 (renamed from 教育部考试中心 in February 2022) |
| **Website** | https://www.neea.edu.cn/ |

NEEA administers over **30 national examinations for 30+ million test-takers annually**, including the gaokao's National Matriculation English Test (NMET), the College English Test (CET-4 and CET-6, which together attract **~18 million test-takers per year**), and the Public English Test System (PETS). It also administers foreign tests in China including TOEFL, GRE, IELTS, and JLPT. NEEA developed the China's Standards of English Language Ability (CSE), initiated in 2014 per State Council directive.

## Conclusion: one ministry, two transformations

China's AI education and language education landscapes share a common center of gravity: the **Ministry of Education**. For AI, the MOE translates top-level State Council strategy into concrete curricula, pilot programs, and digitalization platforms, while MOST provides the ethical and research-funding framework and the CAC controls which AI tools reach classrooms. For language education, the MOE's embedded State Language Commission standardizes Putonghua and English proficiency scales domestically, while CLEC and CIEF project Chinese language education globally through the post-Hanban institutional structure.

The two policy areas are converging. The MOE's 2025 digitalization guidelines explicitly call for AI-powered language learning tools, and the State Council's "AI Plus" framework includes education—language instruction among its targets—as a key sector for transformation by 2035. The most notable divergence is pace: **AI education policy has accelerated dramatically since 2024**, with mandatory K-12 integration now underway, while language education reform has been **evolutionary rather than revolutionary**, with English retaining its full gaokao weight despite years of public debate about reducing it.
